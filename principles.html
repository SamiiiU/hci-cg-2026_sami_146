Human Memory Limits and Chunking (Chapter 3)

Human short-term (working) memory has a limited capacity. A classic observation is that people can retain about 7 ± 2 items, or more accurately, 7 ± 2 chunks of information.

Design implication: Interfaces should minimize memory load by grouping related information into meaningful chunks rather than presenting long unstructured sequences.

Example: Grouped menu options or step-by-step task flows reduce cognitive overload during interaction.

Feedback and Closure Effects (Chapter 3, Page# 155)

Users often experience a sense of closure after completing a sub-task. Once this mental “end” is reached, attention drops and important steps may be forgotten.

Design implication: Critical confirmations or required actions should occur before the user reaches a psychological sense of completion.

Poorly timed feedback can lead to errors even when the interface is otherwise usable.

Evaluation: Why Interfaces Must Be Tested (Chapter 9)

Evaluation is a fundamental activity in HCI. It is used to determine whether a system supports user tasks effectively and delivers an acceptable user experience.

  • Assess usability and learnability
  • Identify usability problems
  • Compare alternative designs

Evaluation ensures that design decisions are grounded in evidence rather than assumptions.
